Mental Health Trends in Tech: How Companies Can Help

Kaylynn Crawford 2016

Visualizations and analysis on the prevalence of mental health issues, ease of access to resources, and company practices that encourage good mental health.
Data visualization and machine learning project for SDS 136 and SDS 293. Smith College 2016.

Prevalence of Mental Health Disorders in Tech

  • 40% of survey respondents have a mental disorder
  • 71% who responded said that their mental disorder affects their productivity

575 people out of the 1433 respondents say they currently have a mental disorder. This is 40% of the survey population. Of the 287 people who answered the next question, 71% say that their mental disorder affects their productivity. Obviously, this is something that companies should address, for both altruistic and business reasons.


Role of Tech Companies in Mental Health Issues

  • Only 46% of respondents know that they have coverage for mental health
  • 54% of respondents have either seen or experienced a negative response to mental health issues

Companies can play either a positive or a negative role in the mental health of their employees. For example, by providing insurance that covers mental health concerns as well as physical health issues, companies can encourage employees to get treatment if necessary.
On the other hand, companies that discourage taking days off for mental health, discourage communication, and don't provide mental health benefits can have a detrimental affect on the mental health of their employees.
As you can see from the graphs, under half of survey respondents knew that their insurance covered mental health benefits, and responses were very mixed regarding whether employees felt that they could take time off without repercussions.

Best Ways for Companies to Help

Overview

This dataset contains information on the respondent's mental health and the attitudes of their company to mental health issues. Using this information, my goal is to determine what a company can do in order to encourage employees to get treatment.
To do this, I built a classification tree with "Treatment" as a response, and figured out which factors best determined if a person with a mental health disorder was receiving treatment.

Data & Methods

I used Python and the sklearn library to clean and analyze the data. First, I read in the CSV file I'd gotten from Kaggle, then I cleaned up the data by removing unnecessary rows and changing the types of the variables from object to category. Since my goal is to predict when people with mental health disorders get treatment, I also removed all the rows where the respondent does not have a mental disorder.
Next, I removed the columns where over 400 people did not respond to the question. Then, I removed any rows left with NaN values. At this point, I have a data set with 381 responses, all with a mental health disorder, and no NaN values.
Then I turned the categorical variables into binary variables using one-hot encoding because Python's decision tree method only takes numerical values. One-hot encoding does not impose ordering on the data.
Lastly, I created the decision tree with a max depth of 6 and displayed the results.

Results

However, the six level tree didn't isolate what the most important factors were in encouraging someone to get treatment. So I modified the code and created a tree with only three levels, to better identify the main factors.
The resulting tree is displayed below. Click to enlarge. The most determinative factor seems to be whether or not the individual is diagnosed, which makes sense. Of those not diagnosed, only 14% are receiving treatment, even though all said that they had a mental disorder.
Beyond that, the most important factors are whether the individual has resources to learn about mental health concerns and options for seeking help, and whether the individual believes that their coworkers would view them differently because of their mental health issues.
The three level tree correctly classified 86.7% of responses and, when split into a training set and a test set, could predict "Treatment" with 91% accuracy. The six level tree correctly classified 91% of responses and predicted "Treatment" with 93% accuracy.


Conclusions & Future Work

Besides a diagnosis, the factors that are most important are having resources, and having supportive coworkers. For companies, the take aways from this should be to encourage employees not to judge others for any mental health disorders, and to provide resources in the form of both insurance and information.
If I had more time, I would definitely try the same analysis in R. The function that creates a decision tree in Python does not accept categorical variables, so the results were harder to interpret than I wanted.
I also think that the results would be more meaningful if there was a better way to deal with the fact that many questions had about five possible responses. I think this muddled the tree and made it harder to interpret.
When the data was split into a training and a test set, a six layer tree had a 93% accuracy rate for determining whether an individual received treatment. This indicates that there are universal factors that can help encourage good mental health practices. Hopefully the survey by OSMI and research done with the resulting data helps to inform companies about good mental health policies.
